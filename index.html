How Automation Supercharged Ransomware in 2025

Attackers didn’t get smarter—they got faster. An inside look at the industrialized supply chain of Initial Access Brokers.
DarkWebIQ Intelligence Team November 21, 2025 • 2 min read

We don’t just watch the perimeter. We infiltrate. By embedding long-standing undercover personas into Initial Access Broker (IAB) markets, we observe the supply chains that feed the ransomware ecosystem before encryption ever happens.

The data from 2025 paints a stark picture: the market is no longer just growing; it is scaling vertically.

The Supply Shock

Throughout 2025, the volume of access offers—a direct proxy for global ransomware supply—exploded. This wasn’t the steady climb of previous years. It was a shock to the system.

In April 2025 alone, we witnessed a 278% year-over-year increase in access offers. This flood of supply created a "buyer's market" for ransomware affiliates, driving down the average cost per victim while driving up the frequency of attacks.

Automation, Not Sophistication

There is a tendency to ascribe near-magical capabilities to threat actors, especially with the rise of LLMs. But the reality inside the access markets is more mundane—and more dangerous.

Attackers didn’t get smarter. They got faster. The explosion in supply is driven by the automation of three legacy techniques: brute forcing, credential parsing, and validation.

Tools like TMChecker and Confucius now validate access like a factory processes parts—automatically, continuously, and at scale. The modern IAB doesn't manually test logins; they run scripts that churn through thousands of credentials derived from infostealer logs.

"Offense = AI for scale plus humans for persuasion."

AI's role here is acceleration, not novelty. It speeds up reconnaissance and lure writing, but most AI usage in the dark web simply mirrors common, licit tooling.

The Myth of Targeting

One of the most persistent myths in cybersecurity is that ransomware operators carefully select their targets based on industry. Our data suggests otherwise.

Across 10 months of interception data, there is no coherent pattern in industry targeting. Education spikes one month; business services the next; manufacturing follows. Initial Access Brokers sell whatever they can brute force. It is a targets-of-opportunity economy.

They are also knocking on the same doors. In January and October alike, VPNs (specifically Fortinet, SonicWall, and Cisco) dominated the access market, with RDWeb and RDP as steady runners-up.

The New Bottleneck: Human Trust

As defenders have successfully pushed security to the "human firewall"—implementing MFA, live verification, and behavioral analytics—pure automation is hitting a wall. It can crack the password, but it often cannot cross the last mile of human verification.

This bottleneck has given rise to Social-Engineering-as-a-Service (SEaaS).

Organized call centers run by threat actors are now turning cheap, automated logins into trusted session tokens. Groups like Scattered Spider have become the "boy bands" of this model, focusing on outcomes rather than malware sophistication.

Key Takeaways

  • Ransomware is rising because supply exploded. We saw 50–100% YoY growth almost every month in 2025.
  • Attackers aren’t more sophisticated, just scaled. They use the same access vectors with higher throughput.
  • Industry doesn’t matter. Attacks are opportunistic; the pie chart of victims shows high variance and no pattern.
  • AI accelerates, it doesn't invent. "Vibe-coded" tools are sufficient to scale attacks without novel exploits.
  • Human trust is the critical bottleneck. As automation handles the brute force, SEaaS is emerging to handle the deception.
